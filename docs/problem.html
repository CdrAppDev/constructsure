The Problem

A $603 Billion Industry Operating Without Financial Controls

Residential renovation in the United States moves hundreds of billions of dollars annually through a payment chain with no independent verification, no fund custody, and no audit trails. Commercial construction solved this decades ago with schedules of value, third-party inspections, and surety bonds. Residential construction — $603 billion in 2024 (Harvard JCHS) — still relies on handshakes and trust.

The result is a system where 1 in 10 Americans report being victimized by contractor fraud (Metal Construction News), 70% of contractors regularly face delayed payments (Built, 2025), and project abandonments have climbed 66.5% to record highs (ConstructConnect). Everyone loses. No one has the infrastructure to stop it.

The Evidence

Converging Data from 226 Citations Across Independent Sources

The research validated all 6 hypotheses in a single round — an unusually strong signal. Payment delays cost the construction industry $280 billion annually (PYMNTS/AmEx, 2025). Contractors wait an average of 83 days for payment against 30-day terms (CFMA). The share of contractors facing 30+ day waits has risen from 49% to 82% in just two years (Rabbet, 2024). Each contractor firm burns 60 hours per month — nearly two full work weeks — on payment administration alone (Construction Cost Accounting).

The founder's claim of "tens of billions in annual losses" is conservative. Payment delays alone account for $280B. Post-disaster fraud adds $9.3 billion (NICB). Bid inflation from payment risk adds another 8% on $400B+ in residential spending (Built, 2025).

The Human Cost

Four Stakeholder Groups Trapped in the Same Broken System

Homeowners fund projects with no mechanism protecting their investment. The FTC received 81,900+ scam reports in 2024 alone (FTC via Uken Report). A Colorado homeowner lost her entire retirement savings on a $350K renovation scam. Seventy-five homeowners received a mass email from a bankrupt roofer who collected prepayments from all of them (Bogleheads Forum).

Contractors experience chronic anxiety: "Sleepless nights follow, and over time, stress can lead to burnout" (Contractor Foreman). A Texas GC was forced onto personal credit cards to pay subcontractors while waiting two months on a $50K invoice. Subcontractors have it worst — 88% have rejected projects over payment reliability concerns (PYMNTS/AmEx, 2025), and 77% cover materials out of pocket before reimbursement.

Lenders funding renovation cannot see how funds are being used. Manual draw inspections average 11 days and cost $200-$500 each (Sekady) — providing snapshots, not real-time visibility. AI-generated fraudulent progress photos are now a distinct fraud category (Truepic).

The Ask

Is This Problem Real and Severe Enough to Build On?

The evidence is clear: residential renovation operates without financial controls, and the cost is measurable across all four stakeholder groups. The question at this gate is not whether the problem exists — the data is overwhelming — but whether it is severe enough and structural enough to justify building a solution.

Gate 1 Decision Required: Review the evidence briefing and validation results below. Decide whether to proceed to Market analysis, revise with additional research, or kill the venture.
01 / Executive Summary

The Validated Problem

Three dimensions of a structural gap in residential renovation financial controls.

Structural Gap

No Independent Controls

Commercial construction mandates SOV frameworks, third-party inspections, surety bonds, and escrow. Residential renovation — at $603B annually — has none as standard practice. Platforms like Buildertrend and CoConstruct handle invoicing but not fund custody or verification.

Financial Impact

$280B+ Annual Losses

Payment delays cost $280B annually across construction. Post-disaster fraud adds $9.3B. Bid inflation from payment risk adds 8% on $400B+ in residential spending. Fraud cases have increased 60%, with median losses of $250K per incident.

Human Impact

Four Groups Suffering

Homeowners face 1-in-10 fraud risk. Contractors burn 60 hrs/month on payment admin. Subcontractors cover 77% of materials out-of-pocket. Lenders get 11-day-old snapshots instead of real-time visibility. Every party prices in mistrust.

04 / First Principles

Six Principles from Evidence

Each principle passed 5 tests: evidence-grounded, denial-tested, derivation-rich, non-obvious, and design-constraining.

P1

Payment Without Verification Is Payment Without Accountability

SOV frameworks exist in commercial construction but not residential. No standardized completion criteria. Platforms generate invoices without enforcing verification.

Implication: Every disbursement must be conditioned on independently verified work completion.

P2

Information Asymmetry Determines Who Bears Financial Risk

Homeowners lack expertise to evaluate work. Subcontractors can't see upstream payment status. Lenders get episodic snapshots. Contractors spend 60 hrs/month managing information gaps.

Implication: Solution must provide symmetric, real-time visibility to all stakeholders.

P3

Payment Chain Delays Compound Through Tiers

82% of contractors wait 30+ days. 71% of subs report delayed GC payments. Carrying costs of 6.17%/month compound through each tier. 88% of subs reject projects over payment concerns.

Implication: Solution must accelerate the entire chain, not just one tier.

P4

Trust-Based Controls Fail at Market Scale

$603B market with relationship-based controls. Fraud cases up 60%. Abandonments at record highs. Payment delays worsened from 49% to 82% in two years.

Implication: Controls must be institutional and structural, not dependent on individual trust.

P5

Financial Controls Are a Prerequisite, Not a Consequence

Commercial construction has controls and lower loss rates. Residential lacks controls at $603B scale. Where escrow exists, outcomes are measurably better.

Implication: Controls must be designed into the payment structure from project inception.

P6

Absent Controls Cost Orders of Magnitude More Than Controls

$280B payment delays vs. $400-$725/draw for escrow. $9.3B post-disaster fraud. 8% bid inflation on $400B+ spending. The ~100:1 ratio of losses to control costs proves controls are economically rational.

Implication: Even partial loss reduction generates compelling economics.

05 / Jobs to Be Done

What People Need to Accomplish

5 functional, 3 emotional, and 2 social jobs derived from customer voice evidence.

Functional Jobs

J1 — Verified Payment

Ensure Money Moves Only When Work Is Verified

Done when: Every payment tied to independently verified milestone completion; no disbursement without third-party confirmation.

Evidence: FTC 109K+ scams, Colorado $350K loss, 1-in-10 fraud rate

J2 — Fast Payment

Receive Payment Within Days of Completing Milestones

Done when: Verified work triggers payment release in 1-2 business days; no manual invoice chasing required.

Evidence: 83-day avg collection, 82% wait 30+ days, 76% would discount for faster pay

J3 — Real-Time Visibility

See Verified Progress and Fund Utilization in Real Time

Done when: Dashboard shows verified vs. claimed progress for every active project; no disbursement without matched verification data.

Evidence: 11-day draw process, $200-$500 inspection costs, AI fraud photos

J4 — Dispute Resolution

Resolve Disputes in Days, Not Months

Done when: Complete audit trail accessible to all authorized parties; disputes reference timestamped records, not competing claims.

Evidence: 16.7-month avg dispute resolution, vague contract language

J5 — Lien Management

Satisfy Lien Rights Automatically at Milestones

Done when: Lien waivers collected and matched to payments; clean title at project completion.

Evidence: Mechanics lien foreclosure risk, lien tools designed for commercial only

Blocking

The evidence shows a $603B industry operating without financial controls standard in commercial construction. All 6 hypotheses validated with 226 citations. Four stakeholder groups suffer measurable harm. The question is whether the structural gap and its consequences justify proceeding to Market analysis.

Proceed → Advance to Market workspace. Analyze whether there is a reachable market of sufficient size where ConstructSure can win.
Revise → Return to Problem workspace. Conduct a second research round targeting the gaps: residential-specific fraud data, commercial vs. residential loss rate comparison, firm-size-stratified burden data.
Kill → Archive the venture with documented learnings. Trigger mandatory post-mortem analyzing why the problem, despite data support, is not worth pursuing.

Strategic Decisions

Inform strategy but do not block the next workspace. Later workspaces produce data that makes these choices clearer.

2
Which stakeholder group should the initial product serve first?
Strategic

All four groups (homeowners, contractors, subcontractors, lenders) suffer from absent controls. The product could enter through any of them. Market workspace will analyze which segment is largest, most reachable, and most underserved.

Revisit after Market workspace — customer profiles and segment analysis will identify the beachhead.

Homeowner-first → B2C entry. Homeowners fund renovations and bear fraud risk. Highest emotional urgency but hardest to reach at scale.
Contractor-first → B2B entry. Contractors burn 60 hrs/month on payment admin. Clear ROI argument. 82% open to digital systems.
Lender-first → B2B2C entry. Lenders need real-time visibility. Draw management is familiar. Built Technologies proves the commercial model works.
3
Should the regulatory moat (50-state licensing) be treated as a barrier or an advantage?
Strategic

Money transmitter and escrow licenses are needed in each state. This is why no national residential fund custody platform exists. It could be a fatal barrier to entry or a powerful moat once crossed.

Revisit after Business workspace — risk register and exit landscape will assess regulatory strategy viability.

Moat strategy → Invest in licensing as a competitive advantage. First mover with national coverage wins.
Partnership strategy → Partner with existing licensed entities (banks, escrow companies) to bypass licensing requirements.
Non-custodial approach → Build verification and visibility without holding funds. Avoids licensing entirely but reduces the value proposition.
